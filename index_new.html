Recreating the Past (MAS.S63)

Instructor: Zach Lieberman

MIT Media Lab (Fall 2021)

In Recreating the Past, we studied computational art from past decades and recreated these works with contemporary techniques to gain aesthetic, analytical, and technical knowledge.

Each classroom session investigated a different set of artists that created work with software, either through code or algorithmic processes. We then recreated their artworks from scratch, discussing their approaches, tools, and poetic underpinnings, investigating what modern tools and approaches might offer to historic works. We also looked at what the artwork taught us about algorithmic approaches and conceptual explorations. The class focused on the building blocks of media art and generative design practice: algorithmic composition, typography, imaging, computer vision, audio analysis/synthesis, and interaction design.

Repetition and Angles: John Whitney

John Whitney (1917â€“1995) was an American animator, composer, and inventor widely regarded as a father of computer animation. Raised in Pasadena and trained in music at Pomona College and in Paris, he began making abstract films in the 1940s with his brother James. Using repurposed World War II anti-aircraft equipment, he built a mechanical analog computer that let him choreograph complex visual motion frame by frame.

Whitney's mature work fused musical structure with what he called "visual harmony." MATRIX III (1972) distills these ideas into a hypnotic study of triangles, squares, and hexagons whose motion follows invisible Lissajous paths across the frame. Set to Terry Riley's Rainbow in Curved Air, the film exemplifies Whitney's belief that computer-generated motion could act as a kind of visual music, uniting time, space, and perception.

Pattern and Color: Bridget Riley

Bridget Riley (b. 1931, London) is a leading figure in Op Art, renowned for paintings that make flat surfaces seem to pulse, tilt, or shimmer. Trained at Goldsmiths and the Royal College of Art, she shifted in the late 1950s from Post-Impressionist, figurative work to a stripped-down language of lines, grids, and curves, shaped by her study of Georges Seurat and modernist abstraction. Early black-and-white works like Movement in Squares (1961) turned perception itself into her subject.

Her reputation crystallized with MoMA's 1965 exhibition The Responsive Eye, where Riley's painting Current became an icon of Op Art. She soon introduced color, developing stripe and curve compositions that use finely tuned chromatic contrasts to suggest rhythm, space, and movement without depicting objects. Over seven decades, working between London, Cornwall, and France, Riley has remained focused on how color and form act on the eye, insisting that the viewer completes the work.

Pixels: Ken Knowlton

Ken Knowlton (1931â€“2022) was an American computer scientist, artist, and pioneer of computer animation. After studying engineering physics at Cornell and completing a PhD at MIT, he joined Bell Labs in the early 1960s, where he developed BEFLIX, one of the first programming languages for computer animation, and later EXPLOR and other graphics systems.

He collaborated with artists such as Stan VanDerBeek and Lillian Schwartz on experimental films, and co-created the landmark computer artwork Studies in Perception I with Leon Harmon. In later years, he built intricate mosaic portraits from thousands of symbols or images, exploring how code, perception, and visual patterning intersect.

Moving Image: Camille Utterback

Camille Utterback (1970â€“ ) is an American artist and programmer known for pioneering interactive and digital art that links human movement to computational systems. Initially trained as a painter, she creates installations where viewers' gestures generate shifting visual fields, emphasizing the body's role in a screen-saturated world. A MacArthur Fellow, she teaches at Stanford and exhibits internationally in museums, galleries, and public spaces.

In Text Rain (1999), created with Romy Achituv, visitors see their own black-and-white silhouettes onscreen as colored letters fall like rain around them. The letters "land" on darker areas of the image, collecting on outstretched arms or other silhouettes until words and lines from a poem emerge. This p5.js filter recreates the iconic "rain" from the original Matrix film, released in the same year as Text Rain.
